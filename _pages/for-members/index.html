Legislative Update

ACNM – Legislative Committee

Summary – 2017 Session Wrap Up

  1. Nursing
    1. HB 253 – Renewal of Licenses – CEUs: Allows renewal of license with CUEs. ACNM Supported. Bill Passed.
    2. HB 482/SB 385 – Board of Nursing: The Board’s clean up bill. Includes repeal of CNM formulary provision. Expands APRN representation from 1 to 2 seats, with both seats being open to any APRN. Adds a new seat open to any LPN, RN, or APRN. ACNM supported. Bill passed.
    3. HB 1632 – Birth Certificates – Out of Institutions:Requires DHMH to allow CNMs and DEMs to register birth certificates, just as in institutions. ACNM supported. Bill passed.
    4. SB 41 – Nursing Licensure Compact: Revises model language so that Maryland may remain in nursing compact. ACNM supported. Bill passed.
  2. Women’s Health/Reproductive Health
    1. HB 214 - Discrimination in Employment - Conditions Related to Pregnancy or Childbirth: Expands requirements for employer accommodation of conditions related to pregnancy and childbirth, including breast feeding. ACNM supported. Bill did not pass.
    2. HB 255/SB 349 – Rape Kits: Requires the State to have a uniform policy about the destruction of rape kits, which must be preserved for 20 years. ACNM supported. The bill passed.
    3. HB 613/SB 363 – Pharmacists Prescribe Contraception: Authorizes pharmacists to prescribe contraceptive medications if meeting Board of Pharmacy requirements for training and protocols. Board must consult with ACNM and other stakeholders. To be implemented in January 2019. ACNM supported. Bill Passed.
    4. HB 616/SB 232 – Pregnant and Parenting Students: Establishes minimal standards to ensure pregnant and parenting students receive excused absences from public schools. ACNM supported. Bill passed.
    5. HB 775/SB 600 – Maternal Mental Health: Requires DHMH to work with stakeholders to develop best practices regarding screening and treatment. Best practices will be disseminated to providers. ACNM supported. Bill Passed.
    6. HB 1083/SB 1081 – Family Planning – Continuity of Services: Requires the State to backfill any federal funds cut from Planned Parenthood. ACNM supported. Bill passed.
    7. HB 1205 – Higher Education – Emergency Contraception: Requires public higher education institutions to make emergency contraception available to students. ACNM supported. Bill did not pass.
    8. SB 27 – Substance Exposed Newborns: Requires providers to report new moms for substance exposed infants, even when caused by a legal prescription or treatment. ACNM opposed. Bill failed.
  3. Health Reform
    1. HJ 9 – The Affordable Care Act: A Maryland General Assembly resolution that directs Congress and the Governor to protect the Affordable Care Act. ACNM supported. Bill passed.
    2. SB 571 – Commission to Protect the Affordable Care Act: Creates a 3-year commission to monitor and address issues related to repeal and replace proposals for the Affordable Care Act. ACNM supported. Bill passed.
  4. Clinical Practice
    1. HB 1432 – Prescribing Opioids – Limitations: Requires prescribers to prescribe the lowest dose and shortest duration possible for opioids. Also requires provider to follow applicable national guidelines. ACNM supported. Bill passed.
    2. SB 110 – Expedited Partner Therapy: Clarified that pharmacists can dispense. Also added trichomoniasis to the program. ACNM supported. Bill passed.
  5. Independence of Board of Nursing
    1. HB 628/SB 517 – Supervision of Anticompetitive Decisions: Protects the independence of the health occupation boards. To comply with the U.S. Supreme Court Decision regarding the North Carolina Dental Board case, the bill appoints an independent third party, the Office of Administrative Hearings, to supervise potentially anticompetitive decisions. The alternative was to give the Secretary of Health authority. ACNM supported. Bill passed.
    2. HB 1471/HB 1474 – Health Occupations Boards: Reduced independence of the health occupation board by imposing a super-board/managerial oversight of the boards. ACNM opposed. Bill failed.

Legislative Update

November Legislative Update

Two sets of regulations are currently open for comment, and moving towards approval in Maryland.

The first is the Direct Entry Midwifery Regulations. The law authorizing the practice of CPMs in Maryland under the title Licensed Direct Entry Midwife (LDEM) is very detailed. For this reason, the regulations have many details that are dictated by the law; these cannot be altered at this time. LDEMs who were educated under the PEP process, rather than in a MEAC accredited education program, are completing a bridge program that closely resembles the national bridge certificate program supported by the US MERA process and offered through NARM. When the regulations are approved, Maryland CPMs will be able to be licensed and practice legally in the home setting. Two CNMs serve on the Board of Nursing DEM Advisory Committee. The MD ACNM Affiliate is supporting the regulations.

Second is the birth center regulations. These are being amended to permit the practice of ultrasound in birth centers by CNMs qualified in the procedure. ACNM is also supporting this regulatory change.

The legislative session opens in January. Preliminary work for the session will begin later this fall. Midwives wishing to work on this committee need to be able to participate in regular conference calls before and during session. They may also be called on to participate in lobbying visits or testimony in Annapolis. We are looking for a diverse group. Please contact Colleen Kennedy, Affiliate Chair, or Jan Kriebs, Legislative Co-Chair, to discuss participating.

Legislative Update

Maryland Legislative Blog

by Jan Kriebs, July 2016

This year the ACNM Maryland Legislative Committee and our lobbyist Robyn Elliott of Public Policy Partners provided input to the Maryland Legislature on 35 unique pieces of legislation. Press events, written and oral testimony, office visits to legislators and a last ditch email/phone campaign all helped support the practice of midwifery in Maryland. Thanks to everyone who called and emailed about the Medical Cannabis Act- it is featured this quarter because without the support of our members and members of the broader nursing community it would not have passed. In future posts, we will talk about the Maternity Access Coalition, contraceptive equity, and direct entry midwifery.

Medical Cannabis

Until this year, only physicians could be certified as providers who authorize the use of medical marijuana to a patient. Provider applications will be available soon for midwives and other providers who have prescriptive authority and who want to offer medical marijuana to non-pregnant women. According to the Maryland Medical Cannabis Commission, medical marijuana can be prescribed for chronic diseases or medical problems that cause severe loss of appetite, severe or chronic pain, severe nausea, Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder (PTSD) as well as other conditions. Patients must have a written certification from the provider and have a bona fide relationship with the provider.

Although few midwives will have appropriate patients for medical cannabis, the bill was important to Maryland CNMS as a matter of principle, to preserve full prescriptive authority (i.e., without restrictions compared to other APRNs practicing in primary care settings). The legislation became unexpectedly contentious when a few pharmacists misrepresented both the scope of practice and clinical abilities of CNMs in testimony.

When the final bill passed, CNMs remained in, largely due to three factors. First, the ACNM Affiliate has a lobbyist who works long and hard on our behalf as well as having many relationships and other clients within the larger nursing community. Second, both the Maryland Nurses Association and the Nurse Practitioner Association of Maryland sent out blast emails to members, as did ACNM, urging members to call legislators with a specific, well-crafted message. The message supported midwives and pointed out that nurses make up a large part of the health care community. Third, Delegate Morhaim and Senator Raskin continued their vocal support for CNMs to remain in the bill throughout the process.

More information is available from the Maryland Medical Cannabis Commission website (although it does not yet list CNMs as authorized providers).

HIV Testing in Pregnancy

Third trimester testing for HIV is now required unless the woman opts-out after being offered the test. Only the District of Columbia and Louisiana have higher rates of HIV transmission to women than Maryland does. Best practice is to obtain written consent that includes all tests, and document which tests are declined.

Board of Nursing

A ‘clean-up’ bill for the Board of Nursing removed outdated language and created the opportunity for CNMs and advanced practice nurses to have advisory peer committees. It also established new rules and penalties for being an ‘imposter’ - holding oneself out as legally able to practice without being properly licensed.
